Of course. This is a classic puzzle known as the Tower of Hanoi. The most efficient way to solve it is by using a recursive algorithm.

### The Strategy

To move a stack of $n$ disks from a source peg to a destination peg, we can break the problem down as follows:

1.  Move the top $n-1$ disks from the **source** peg to the **auxiliary** peg.
2.  Move the single largest disk ($n$) from the **source** peg to the **destination** peg.
3.  Move the $n-1$ disks from the **auxiliary** peg to the **destination** peg.

For your puzzle with 12 disks, this means:
- **Source Peg:** 0
- **Destination Peg:** 2
- **Auxiliary Peg:** 1

The total number of moves required for $n$ disks is $2^n - 1$. For 12 disks, this is $2^{12} - 1 = 4096 - 1 = 4095$ moves.

### Exploration with a smaller example (n=2)

Let's apply the strategy to move 2 disks from peg 0 to peg 2.

1.  **Move n-1 (1) disk from source (0) to auxiliary (1):**
    - Move disk 1 from 0 to 1. `[1, 0, 1]`
2.  **Move n (2) disk from source (0) to destination (2):**
    - Move disk 2 from 0 to 2. `[2, 0, 2]`
3.  **Move n-1 (1) disk from auxiliary (1) to destination (2):**
    - Move disk 1 from 1 to 2. `[1, 1, 2]`

The complete list of moves for n=2 is `[[1, 0, 1], [2, 0, 2], [1, 1, 2]]`.
